# Feedback Practice

## Framework: SBI + Request

### Feedback 1: Positive - Jordan's Improvement

**Situation:**
"In yesterday's code review for the payment service..."

**Behavior:**
"...you identified a critical security flaw in the input validation that everyone else had missed."

**Impact:**
"This prevented a potential vulnerability from reaching production and showed real growth in your attention to security concerns."

**Request:**
"I'd love for you to share this approach in our next team meeting - it would help others learn."

---

### Feedback 2: Constructive - Sam's Comfort Zone

**Situation:**
"Over the past quarter, I've noticed in our project assignments..."

**Behavior:**
"...you've consistently taken on familiar tasks rather than volunteering for new challenges like the Kubernetes migration."

**Impact:**
"While your work remains high quality, this pattern limits your growth and means others don't benefit from your experience in new areas."

**Request:**
"For next quarter, I'd like you to lead at least one project outside your comfort zone. Can we discuss which one interests you?"

---

### Feedback 3: Difficult - Riley's Performance

**Situation:**
"Over the past month, specifically in the checkout service project..."

**Behavior:**
"...you've missed two sprint commitments and the code that was delivered had three P2 bugs."

**Impact:**
"The team had to scramble to cover the gaps, and our stakeholders are concerned about timeline reliability."

**Request:**
"I want to support you - can we discuss what's going on and create a plan to get back on track?"

### Feedback 4: Peer Conflict - Morgan and Alex

## Additional Notes

The SBI version of the Riley feedback above is the short in-the-moment version. The Riley situation also needs a planned, sit-down conversation, so I wrote a full difficult conversation script to prepare for it. That script is below, kept separate so the 6 SBI scenarios above match the template format cleanly.

### Riley Difficult Conversation Script (full version)

#### Situation Context

Riley's delivery has been off for about two months. Missed commitments, a few more bugs than Riley's usual standard, and communication gaps with a stakeholder who has raised it to me twice. In our 1:1s, Riley has hinted at a personal situation but has not asked for any adjustment. Waiting is not kind — waiting feels like kindness in the moment but becomes unkind later. I am having this conversation now so we have more options together.

**Setup:**
- Private meeting room, not my desk
- 60 minutes on the calendar
- Calendar title: "Check-in and planning" — nothing alarming
- Short note the day before: "I want to talk about how you are doing and how I can support you. Nothing to worry about going in."

#### Key Messages to Deliver

1. "I have noticed a change, and I am concerned about you, not about your job."
2. "Here is what I am seeing, specifically, and here is the impact on the team."
3. "We need a plan together. I have ideas, but this has to be something you agree to, not something I impose."

#### Anticipated Responses and How I Will Handle Them

**Reaction A — Defensive:** "I am doing my best. The bugs were edge cases."

My response: slow down, do not argue single incidents. "I hear you, and I believe you are doing your best. I am not here to argue about any one bug or meeting. I am here because I see a pattern, and patterns tell me something is harder right now than usual. Can we talk about what has been making things harder?"

**Reaction B — Emotional:** Riley tears up or goes very quiet.

My response: stop pushing, pass the tissues, say nothing for a moment. "Take your time. There is no rush. We have as much time as you need." If Riley wants to stop and come back tomorrow, that is fine, no consequences.

**Reaction C — Shutdown:** Riley goes silent and will not engage.

My response: do not fill the silence with words. Wait. If the silence goes past a minute, one gentle prompt: "I am not going to push. What you tell me in this room stays in this room unless you say otherwise. And if you would rather not talk about the personal side, that is completely okay — we can focus on the work side only."

**Reaction D — Opens up about the personal situation:**

My response: listen, do not try to fix. "Thank you for telling me. That takes a lot. I am not going to pretend I have answers, and I do not want to. What I can do is make work one less pressure while you handle the rest. Here is what I am proposing..."

#### Desired Outcomes

- Riley leaves feeling safe, not judged
- Concrete plan agreed: 70% capacity, weekly 1:1s, flexible hours, EAP offered
- Team workload formally redistributed so nobody burns out quietly
- Stakeholders get a clear, private answer from me on timeline expectations
- HR aware this is a support plan, not a PIP

#### Follow-up Actions

Within 24 hours:
- Written summary sent to Riley in plain language
- Sprint plan updated to 70% capacity
- Auth service on-call quietly moved to Morgan for 8 weeks
- EAP contact shared directly with Riley
- HR informed about the support plan

Week 1: First new weekly 1:1, low-pressure mid-week check-in message.
Week 4: Mid-point review, is the plan working?
Week 8: Formal review together, decide what happens next based on what we both see.

#### What I Will Avoid

- Lecturing Riley
- Comparing Riley to other team members
- Promising things I cannot deliver
- Asking Riley to explain the personal situation in detail
- Leaving the room without a clear next step
